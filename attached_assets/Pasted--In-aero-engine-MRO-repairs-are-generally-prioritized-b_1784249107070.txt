
 
In aero engine MRO, repairs are generally prioritized by safety, regulatory compliance, and contractual requirements, rather than just cost or convenience. While the exact categorization varies between OEMs (such as Rolls-Royce, GE Aerospace, and Pratt & Whitney) and MROs, a typical hierarchy is as follows:

Category 1 – Airworthiness Mandatory

These repairs are essential because the engine would no longer comply with its certified type design or maintenance requirements.

Examples include:

Cracks beyond allowable limits
Out-of-limit dimensions
Failed critical inspections
Non-compliance with the Engine Manual (EM), Component Maintenance Manual (CMM), or approved repair limits
These items must be rectified before return to service.


Category 2 – Life-Limited Parts (LLPs)

LLPs have certified cyclic or hourly life limits.

Examples:

Turbine discs
Compressor discs
Shafts
Certain seals
Once an LLP reaches its approved life limit, it must be replaced—there is no extension without specific regulatory approval.


Category 3 – Safety-Critical Repairs

These involve components whose failure could have catastrophic consequences.

Examples:

Fan containment components
Turbine discs
Main shafts
Bearing support structures
Critical bolted joints
These repairs receive the highest engineering scrutiny and inspection requirements.


Category 4 – Regulatory Requirements

These include:

Airworthiness Directives (mandatory)
Mandatory OEM Service Bulletins
Regulatory findings
Customer contractual requirements
Some Service Bulletins are optional from a regulatory perspective but may be contractually required by the operator.


Category 5 – Functional Restoration

These ensure the engine can meet operational requirements.

Examples:

Tip clearance restoration
Blade replacement
Seal restoration
Combustor repairs
Balance corrections
These repairs help ensure the engine achieves the required thrust, fuel efficiency, vibration levels, and exhaust gas temperature (EGT) margin.


Category 6 – Reliability Improvements

These are often incorporated during overhaul because the engine is already disassembled.

Examples:

Latest approved repair schemes
Durability upgrades
Improved coatings
Modified hardware to reduce future shop visits
These repairs are usually driven by lifecycle cost and fleet reliability rather than immediate airworthiness.


Category 7 – Cosmetic Repairs

Examples:

Paint touch-up
Surface finish improvements
Non-functional appearance defects
These have no impact on the engine’s airworthiness.

How leading MROs prioritise work

In practice, production control often classifies repairs into three operational buckets:

Red (Must Do): Airworthiness, LLPs, safety-critical repairs, mandatory ADs/SBs. These are on the critical path and must be completed before release.
Amber (Should Do): Performance and reliability repairs that improve engine life or efficiency and are normally completed during the shop visit.
Green (Could Do): Opportunistic or cosmetic work that may be deferred if it does not affect safety, compliance, contractual obligations, or agreed performance targets.
This approach helps planners focus engineering resources and material availability on the tasks that most directly affect turnaround time and regulatory compliance while still capturing opportunities to improve long-term engine reliability.
