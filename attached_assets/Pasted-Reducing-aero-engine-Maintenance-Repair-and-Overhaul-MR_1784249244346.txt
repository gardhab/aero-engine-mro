Reducing aero engine Maintenance, Repair and Overhaul (MRO) turnaround time (TAT) requires optimizing the entire value stream rather than just making the shop floor faster. The highest-performing engine MRO organizations focus on removing waiting time, improving planning accuracy, and eliminating rework.

A practical approach is to focus on the following areas, roughly in order of impact:

1. Improve pre-induction planning (highest return)

Many delays begin before the engine reaches the shop.

Best practices include:

Complete digital records before engine arrival.
Predict LLP replacement and repair requirements using fleet data.
Pre-order known material.
Reserve tooling and specialist resources.
Freeze the shop visit scope as early as possible.
This allows work to begin immediately instead of waiting for engineering decisions.


2. Eliminate material shortages

Parts availability is often the single largest contributor to TAT.

Focus on:

Critical spare part buffers
Repair exchange pools
Supplier performance management
Automated reorder points
Visibility of repairable assets
Alternative repair routes where approved
A single missing HPT blade or bearing can delay an entire engine.


3. Improve work scope prediction

Using historical overhaul data and engine health monitoring allows the MRO to predict:

likely scrap rates
expected repairs
LLP replacements
module findings
Predictive analytics means fewer surprises during strip.


4. Reduce waiting—not processing time

In most MROs, only a small fraction of TAT is value-adding work. Engines often spend much longer waiting between operations.

Typical causes include:

Waiting for engineering dispositions
Waiting for inspectors
Waiting for transport
Waiting for cranes
Waiting for repair slots
Waiting for missing paperwork
Reducing these queues often delivers greater gains than making individual tasks faster.


5. Introduce flow-based production

Rather than batching engines, create continuous flow where possible by:

Visual production control
Daily scheduling
Limiting work in progress
Dedicated module flow lines
Pull systems between operations
This reduces congestion and improves predictability.


6. Digital work instructions and OCR

Replacing paper-based processes with digital systems can reduce delays and transcription errors.

Useful technologies include:

OCR for part and serial number capture
Barcode and QR scanning
RFID tracking
Tablet-based work instructions
Digital sign-offs
Automated compliance checks
These also reduce the risk of incorrect part or serial number recording.


7. Improve first-time quality

Every rework loop adds days or weeks.

Focus on:

Standard work
Error-proofing (Poka-Yoke)
Independent quality gates
Digital torque recording
Automated inspection where appropriate
Technician certification
Reducing rework improves both TAT and cost.


8. Optimise repair routing

Not every part requires the same repair route.

Review:

Internal vs external repair decisions
Repair prioritisation
Specialist capacity
Repair batching
Approved alternative repair schemes
A dynamic repair routing process can significantly reduce elapsed time.


9. Use real-time production management

Leading MROs use live operational dashboards showing:

Engine location
Current operation
Time in operation
Queue time
Critical path
Material shortages
Engineering queries
Predicted completion date
This enables proactive intervention before delays escalate.


10. Measure the right KPIs

Rather than focusing solely on total TAT, monitor the drivers of delay:

Total Turnaround Time
Queue Time (% of TAT)
First Pass Yield
Schedule Adherence
Parts Availability
Engineering Query Resolution Time
External Repair Lead Time
Technician Productivity
Resource Utilisation
Work-in-Progress (WIP)
Right First Time
On-Time Engine Delivery
